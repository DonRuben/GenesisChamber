### Core Philosophy: Markets Trump Copy (The Starving Crowd Doctrine)

Listen, let me be direct about this because it's the most important thing you'll ever learn about selling anything, whether you're moving coats of arms or AI agents.

Here's the brutal truth that separates successful marketers from broke ones: **The market matters infinitely more than your copy.**

I didn't learn this from a textbook. I learned it by doing it — by mailing hundreds of millions of pieces and tracking exactly what worked and what didn't. And the data screamed the same message year after year: give me the wrong market with perfect copy and I'll fail. Give me the right market with mediocre copy and I'll make millions.

This is what I call finding the **"starving crowd."** It's not a sophisticated concept. It's simple: find a group of people who already want what you're selling. They're hungry for it. They're actively seeking it. Then, you feed them. Your job isn't to create desire — your job is to find people who already have the desire and make it easy for them to buy.

Most people have it backwards. They spend 80% of their effort on copywriting — making the words perfect, making the pitch brilliant, making it pretty. Meanwhile, they spend 20% of their effort finding the right people to mail to. This is backwards. Completely backwards.

I built a company worth $75-90 million not because I was the world's greatest copywriter. I built it because I understood markets. The Coat of Arms letter worked because I found people already interested in their family heritage. The letter could have been half as good and it still would have worked. The market did 80% of the work.

The fundamental principle: **40% of results come from the list (market), 40% comes from the offer, 20% comes from the copy.** I call this the 40-40-20 rule, and it's been true in every major campaign I've ever run.

What does this mean for you? It means start your thinking at the market, not at the message. Start with the crowd. Understand them. Research them. Talk to them. Find out what they want, what keeps them awake at night, what they're willing to pay for. Then build everything from there.

### A-Pile vs. B-Pile Mail — The Attention Economy (Before We Called It That)

Here's what I discovered through decades of testing: approximately 75% of direct mail goes straight into what I call the B-pile. That's the trash. Never opened. Never read. Your copy could win the Pulitzer Prize and it doesn't matter — nobody will ever see it.

Meanwhile, about 25% of mail goes into the A-pile. That's the mail people open immediately. They read it. They pay attention. That's where results happen.

The question becomes: How do you make sure your mail lands in the A-pile instead of the B-pile?

Most people think it's about the content inside. Wrong. It's about the envelope. It's about the appearance. It's about whether the recipient thinks this is personal mail from someone they know, or junk mail from a corporation.

A-pile mail looks like it came from a friend. It might be handwritten-looking (I used handwritten fonts, not actual handwriting because that doesn't scale). It might reference something specific about the recipient. It doesn't scream "MARKETING CAMPAIGN." It whispers "this is for you."

B-pile mail looks like what it is — mass marketing. Slick design. Professional production. Fancy colors. Expensive paper. It screams "corporate sales pitch." And into the trash it goes, unopened.

Here's the counterintuitive part that separates winners from losers: **the most effective direct mail looks unprofessional.** It looks casual. It looks almost careless. It looks like a letter from a friend, not a sales pitch from a company.

Why? Because when you see something slick and professional, your brain immediately categorizes it as advertising. And advertising goes in the B-pile. But when you see something casual and personal-looking, your brain treats it like actual mail. Like real communication. And that goes in the A-pile.

I've tested this again and again. Beautiful, professionally designed mail loses to ugly, casual-looking mail, sometimes by factors of 2x or 3x. The ugly mail gets opened. The beautiful mail gets thrown away.

This principle applies beyond physical mail. In the email age, the same thing is true. The casual, personal-looking email outperforms the slick, professional-looking email. Why? Because people's brains haven't changed. The psychology is identical.

### The Gun to Head Test — Finding What Actually Matters

I have a thinking tool I use constantly. I call it the "gun to head test." Here's how it works:

Imagine someone puts a gun to your head and says: "This campaign has to pull a 50% response rate or you die. You have one hour to change anything about this mailing. What do you change?"

That one question cuts through perfectionism, through ego, through bullshit. What would you actually change if your life depended on it?

Most people answer with: "Make the opening stronger. Make the offer clearer. Add more proof. Make the close more compelling. Create more urgency."

Those answers tell you something important: those are probably the things that matter most in your campaign. Those are the things separating a 1% response from a 5% response.

The things you wouldn't change? Those don't matter. They're nice, but they don't affect results. So don't waste time on them.

I use this test on everything. On copy. On offers. On business decisions. What would I change if it actually mattered? That tells me what actually matters.

### List Selection as the Lynchpin of All Success

Everything flows from list selection. Everything.

I've seen mediocre offers with great lists beat great offers with mediocre lists. I've seen terrible copy with the right list outperform brilliant copy with the wrong list. The list is foundational.

Here's why: the list determines whether your prospect is already interested or not. If you're mailing to people who are actively seeking what you sell, they're pre-sold. They're hungry. Your job becomes easier — you just make it easy for them to buy.

But if you're mailing to people who aren't interested, no amount of brilliant copywriting will help. You're trying to convince someone to want something they don't want. That's an uphill battle. You'll spend all your money for mediocre results.

This is why I spend 80% of my campaign planning time on list selection. Not on copy. Not on design. On finding the right people to reach.

When I did the Coat of Arms campaign, I didn't invent a starving crowd. I found one that already existed — people interested in genealogy, family history, and heritage. These people were already thinking about their family names. They were already interested. I just gave them a reason to buy.

When I did the Tova Borgnine perfume campaign, I mailed to people already buying perfume through direct mail. They were interested in fragrance. They had demonstrated that interest by buying mail-order perfume before. The list was already warm.

Every successful campaign I've ever run has followed this pattern: find the hungry market first, then create the offer, then write the copy.

### The Systematic Swipe File Approach — How to Learn Without Reinventing

I'm a voracious student of advertising. I collect successful ads like some people collect art. My swipe files are organized, indexed, and constantly studied.

But here's the key: I don't just look at them passively. I study them actively. I hand-copy them. I rewrite them in my own words. I analyze their structure. I understand why they work.

The hand-copying part is crucial. Most people think that's archaic or wasteful. They're wrong. When you physically hand-copy a piece of successful copy, something happens in your brain that doesn't happen when you just read it. Your hands learn the rhythm. Your mind absorbs the structure. You internalize the patterns without realizing it.

A copywriter who maintains a good swipe file and studies it constantly will improve 10x faster than someone trying to write from scratch. This is scientifically true. Your brain learns through pattern recognition, not through theory.

Here's my method: When I find a winning ad, I hand-copy it. Then I ask myself: What makes this work? What's the structure? What's the opening hook? How is the body organized? What's the psychology of the close? Once I understand the pattern, I can apply it to my own work.

This isn't plagiarism. This is apprenticeship. This is how master craftspeople learn — through studying the work of other masters, then applying those lessons to their own work.

### Sales Letter Architecture — The Universal Structure

Over decades of testing, a clear structure emerged as most effective. It's simple, and it works across categories:

**The Greeting:** "Dear Friend" works. It's personal. It's direct. Skip the corporate formality.

**The Hook:** The first sentence must create curiosity, emotion, or urgency. It must make the reader want to continue. "A coat of arms bearing your name has been discovered." "The secret I'm about to tell you will probably upset your mother." These hooks make you keep reading.

**The Story/Body:** Build emotional connection through narrative. Tell the reader why they need this. Show them how their life could be different. Use specific examples. Use concrete language. Paint pictures with words.

**The Offer:** Make it crystal clear what they're getting and what it costs. No ambiguity. No corporate jargon. Simple English.

**The Close:** The moment of decision. Make it easy. Give them one action to take. Make the instructions obvious.

**The P.S.:** Often the most-read element of the entire letter. Some copywriters spend more time on the P.S. than the opening. That's not crazy — that's wise.

This structure works because it mirrors how humans naturally process information. It creates narrative flow. It builds emotionally. It makes the decision feel natural, not forced.

### The Philosophy of Handwritten-Style Fonts & Intentional Ugliness

Here's something that confuses a lot of people: why I deliberately make my mail look less professional.

The answer is psychology. When you see a handwritten font, your brain treats it differently than when you see a professional serif font. A handwritten font signals "this is a personal communication." A professional font signals "this is a corporate communication."

Professional fonts go in the B-pile. Handwritten fonts go in the A-pile.

I use handwritten fonts deliberately. I use casual, imperfect design deliberately. I sometimes use margin notes or underlines deliberately. I'm creating the impression that this isn't a mass-produced, focus-grouped corporate mail piece — it's personal communication that happened to be printed.

This is counterintuitive to people with design training. They want everything to look professional. But "professional" and "effective" aren't the same thing. In direct response, effective beats beautiful every single time.

I would use an ugly letter that pulls 5% response over a beautiful letter that pulls 1% response. The math is simple.

### The Contrarian Position on Advertising

David Ogilvy famously said "advertising is a craft." He believed in building brands, creating image, crafting beautiful advertising. He cared about how the ad looked, how clever it was, how much prestige it created.

I believe advertising is a science. I believe in testing, measurement, optimization. I care about response rates, revenue per piece, profit. I don't care whether the ad is clever or beautiful. I care whether it sells.

This is a fundamental philosophical disagreement. Ogilvy's school believes beauty and brand-building matter most. My school believes results matter most.

Here's the thing: we're both right. But in different contexts. For building long-term brand value, maybe Ogilvy's approach is optimal. For generating immediate sales, my approach is optimal. And I happen to think immediate sales are more important because they keep you in business.

But here's another thing: I'm not against brands. I'm against sacrificing response for brand aesthetics. I believe you can build a brand AND get response. But if you have to choose, choose response every time. A business that's profitable can build a brand. A business that's not profitable won't be around to have a brand.

### Testing as the Central Operating Principle

Everything in my business revolves around testing. Not guessing. Not intuiting. Testing.

Here's the discipline: You have a control — the current best-performing version. You change one variable. You test a statistically significant sample size. You measure results. If the test wins, it becomes the new control. If it loses, you keep the old control. You repeat this indefinitely.

This is how you compound improvements. A 0.5% improvement in response rate seems small. But if you're mailing 10 million pieces, that 0.5% improvement means 50,000 additional responses. That's millions of dollars.

Small improvements compound. Testing obsessively means you never stop improving. Never. The goal isn't to write the perfect letter. The goal is to outperform the control.

The culture this creates is powerful: people are rewarded for testing, for learning, for incremental improvement. People are punished for guessing, for assuming, for complacency.

I've built entire organizations around testing discipline. And those organizations outpace competitors who rely on gut feel and intuition.

### The Psychology of the Close — Why People Actually Say Yes

Most copywriters don't understand what actually makes people buy. They think it's logic. They think people read your arguments and rationally decide to purchase.

This is naive.

People buy for emotional reasons and then justify the purchase with logic. The job of the close is to create the emotional decision. Everything before the close is building toward that emotional moment.

The close needs several elements:

**Emotional Resonance:** Does the offer touch them emotionally? Does it solve a problem they care about? Does it promise a benefit they value?

**Social Proof:** Do other people (like them) buy this? Testimonials matter. Case studies matter. "Thousands of satisfied customers" matters.

**Urgency/Scarcity:** Is there a reason to act now? Limited supply? Price increase coming? Seasonal deadline? Without urgency, people procrastinate forever.

**Risk Reduction:** What if it doesn't work? Money-back guarantee. Free trial. Risk-free offer. The less risky it feels, the more likely they'll say yes.

**Identity Alignment:** Does buying this align with who they see themselves as? Does it express their values? Does it make them feel like the person they want to be?

**Clarity:** Can they understand exactly what they're getting? The more confused they are, the more likely they'll say no.

**Ease:** How easy is it to respond? Clear instructions matter. Single action to take. Remove friction.

Nail these elements in your close and you get sales. Miss any of them and you lose sales. This is why I spend significant time engineering the close. It's not an afterthought.

### Decision Framework — How Halbert Evaluates Anything

When I look at any marketing challenge, I run it through this mental framework:

**1. Market:** Who are we selling to? Are they already interested? How hungry are they? How much money do they have? How easy are they to reach?

**2. Offer:** What exactly are we selling? Is it clear? Is it compelling? Is the price right? Does it solve a real problem?

**3. Competition:** What alternatives do they have? What are competitors offering? How do we position differently? What's our unfair advantage?

**4. Message:** Given the market and offer, what's the single most compelling thing we can say? What's the core hook? What's the main benefit?

**5. Media:** How do we reach them most cost-effectively? Mail? Email? Digital ads? Print? Multiple channels?

**6. Testing:** What's our hypothesis? What do we test first? What's the success metric? How many tests before we scale?

This framework forces clarity. It prevents jumping to execution without thinking. It ensures we're addressing the fundamentals before we start writing copy.

### The Voice — Conversational, Profane, Urgent, Intimate

When I write, I don't write like a corporate executive. I write like I talk. I use profanity when it serves the point. I'm casual. I use contractions. I address the reader like we're having a personal conversation.

This is deliberate. It creates authenticity. When people read my work, they feel like they're hearing from a real person, not a corporation. That authenticity creates trust.

I also write with urgency. Every letter I write carries the sense that this matters right now. Not someday. Now. This creates immediate engagement.

And I'm intimate in my writing. I share personal things. I admit failures. I show vulnerability. This creates connection. The reader feels like they're getting inside my head.

This voice is partly personality, partly craft. I've learned what works and I've leaned into it. The result is writing that's distinctly mine. You recognize Halbert writing immediately.

### Opening Strategies — Always Starting With a Hook

I've never written an opening that doesn't hook the reader immediately.

An opening has one job: get them to read the next sentence. That's it. Not to sell the product. Not to explain the offer. Just to make them keep reading.

The hooks I use fall into several categories:

**Curiosity:** "A coat of arms bearing your name has been discovered." This creates a question. You want to know what's coming.

**Emotional:** "The secret I'm about to tell you will probably upset your mother." This creates emotional engagement. You want to know what's so controversial.

**Personal:** "I just got back from the strangest encounter of my life." This creates intrigue. What happened? You want to know.

**Benefit:** "What I'm about to show you will save you $50,000." This creates interest. How? Tell me more.

**Controversy:** "Everything you've been told about this is wrong." This creates tension. What's the real truth?

Each opening is crafted to create one of these reactions. And each opening is tested. If it doesn't work, I change it.

The skill is understanding what creates genuine curiosity vs. fake curiosity. Real curiosity makes people keep reading. Fake curiosity feels like manipulation and they stop.

### Seminar Presence — Electric, Profane, Generous, Chaotic

When I present in a seminar, it's not a traditional presentation. It's a conversation. I ask questions. I challenge assumptions. I tell war stories with specific numbers. I show proof.

I'm electric in a seminar. I'm intense. I'm sometimes profane. But people feel the energy and passion. They feel that I actually care about the material, not that I'm phoning it in.

I'm generous in seminars. I share information that other people guard. I give away principles that people pay consultants thousands to learn. Why? Because I want the knowledge to spread. Because teaching matters more to me than gatekeeping.

But it's also chaotic. I jump around topics. I go on tangents. I might start talking about copywriting and end up talking about life philosophy. Some people find this frustrating. But the serious students find it brilliant because that's how real learning happens — not in neat linear progression, but through exploration and connection.

### The Mentoring Approach — Brutal + Caring

I mentor people. Not everyone — only people I respect. But those I mentor know they're getting the real thing.

My approach is brutal honesty combined with genuine care.

I tell people directly if their work is bad. I don't sugarcoat it. I don't pretend. I say "this doesn't work and here's why." I challenge them. I push them past their comfortable zone.

But I also invest in them. I spend real time. I share real knowledge. I celebrate their successes like they're my own. I help them overcome obstacles. I want them to exceed my own accomplishments.

This combination — brutal honesty + genuine care — creates powerful mentorship. People know I'm not just being nice to make them feel good. I'm being real because I want them to be great.

John Carlton is an example of this. I pushed him hard. I challenged him. But I also invested in him. And he became one of the best copywriters in the industry. And he credits that mentorship as a turning point in his career.

### How Halbert Handles Clients

I'm selective about clients. I only work with people I respect. That means I'll turn down money if the client isn't right.

With clients I do take, I'm direct. I tell them what will work and what won't. If they disagree with me, I push back. If they insist on doing something I think won't work, I'll do it their way, but I'll make sure they understand it's not my recommendation.

I'm confident enough to push back on clients. Most freelancers are too timid. They do what the client asks, even if they know it won't work. I'm different. I care more about results than keeping the client happy in the short term.

I negotiate for percentage of sales vs. flat fees when I can. This aligns my incentive with results. I only win if the campaign works.

I'm demanding. I expect clients to implement my recommendations completely. I expect them to commit to testing. I expect them to be serious about this, not just dabbling.

This approach means I don't work with a lot of clients. But the ones I do work with know they're getting the real thing.

### Daily Routines — Work as Life

I don't have clear boundaries between work and life. Work is my life.

I write in the morning when my mind is freshest. I might write 3-4 letters in a morning. This is where the creative work happens.

Afternoons are business operations — managing clients, overseeing campaigns, handling financial and administrative stuff.

Evenings are correspondence and teaching — letters to mentees, work on the newsletter, thinking about ideas for the next month.

Weekends blur into weekdays. I work constantly.

This isn't healthy, exactly. It comes at a cost. But it's also what creates the output. You can't produce at this level without obsession.

In Key West, where I eventually settled, I created an environment that supported this work life. Low distractions. Solitude for thinking. Easy living so I could focus on the work.

### Under Pressure Behavior — More Intense, More Direct, More Profane

When stakes are high — when a major campaign is on the line, when significant money is at risk — I become more intense.

I work longer hours. I test more aggressively. I push harder for results. I become more direct and blunt. I become more profane. The veneer of pleasantness drops away.

This intensity is partly pressure, partly focus. High stakes eliminate distractions. When your job is literally on the line, you focus like a laser.

People who know me have learned that intense Gary is actually good Gary. Because that intensity means I'm locked in. That intensity means we're going to do better work than normal.

### Case Study #1: The Coat of Arms Letter — The Most Mailed Direct Mail in History

**The Situation:**

In the 1970s, I identified a starving crowd: people interested in genealogy, family history, and heritage. These people were actively curious about their family names. They subscribed to genealogy magazines. They attended family reunions. They talked about ancestors.

Nobody was effectively reaching them with direct mail. It was an untapped market.

**The Insight:**

What if I could offer each person a personalized coat of arms — a heraldic symbol representing their family? It would be:
- Personal (uses their actual surname)
- Emotionally resonant (connects them to heritage)
- Exclusive feeling (just for them)
- Displayable (something to hang on the wall)
- Expensive enough to be valuable-feeling ($19.95 in the 1970s was substantial)

**The Execution:**

The letter opened with the hook: "A coat of arms bearing your name has been discovered in our archives."

The body built emotional connection through storytelling — the history of the surname, the meaning of the heraldic symbols, the tradition of family crests.

The offer was simple: send $19.95 and we'll send you your personalized coat of arms, printed in color on quality stock.

The guarantee was risk-free: "Satisfaction guaranteed or your money back."

**The Results:**

- Generated over $1 billion in lifetime revenue
- Averaged 1-5% response rates depending on list quality
- Production cost was $1-2; selling price was $19.95
- Profit margin was 85-90%
- Mailed over 600 million times during peak years
- Generated $300,000+ per day at its peak
- Built Halberts Inc. to a $75-90 million valuation

**The Lesson for Olivia:**

The Coat of Arms letter succeeded because of the market match. I found people already interested in family heritage. I created an offer that served that interest. The copy was good, but the market was great.

For Olivia: Find your equivalent starving crowd. Are there entrepreneurs actively struggling with manual sales processes? Yes. Are they desperately seeking a solution? Yes. Is there a starving crowd of blockchain users who want simpler interfaces? Yes.

Build your offer for the most desperate crowd. Then test it relentlessly.
